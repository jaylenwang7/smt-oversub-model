# Reported performance gains from simultaneous multithreading across vendors and academic studies

## Executive summary

Simultaneous multithreading (SMT)-marketed as Hyper‑Threading on many Intel CPUs-is primarily positioned as a **throughput** feature: by letting multiple hardware threads share one physical core's execution resources, the core can keep more units busy when one thread is stalled (for example on cache misses). Across vendor materials and empirical academic studies, **reported gains most commonly cluster around +10% to +60% aggregate throughput**, but the spread is wide and strongly workload-dependent. [\[1\]](https://www.intel.com/content/dam/www/public/us/en/documents/white-papers/virtualization-xeon-core-count-impacts-performance-paper.pdf)

Vendor-facing language usually emphasizes "performance" or "throughput" without pinning down whether it is **single-thread speed**, **system throughput**, or **application-level request rate**-and when a percent is given, it is often framed as "up to" (peak) rather than typical/median behavior. [\[2\]](https://www.intel.com/content/dam/www/public/us/en/documents/research/2002-vol06-iss-1-intel-technology-journal.pdf)

Across the vendor sources gathered here:

- Intel[\[3\]](https://www.ibm.com/support/pages/power-cpu-memory-affinity-3-scheduling-processes-smt-and-virtual-processors) materials include claims such as ~**30%** "performance gain" in a technical guide, **up to 30%** on "common server application benchmarks" in Intel Technology Journal, and **10%-20%** in a virtualization-focused white paper (vs. the same server without HT). [\[4\]](https://read.seas.harvard.edu/cs161/2022/pdf/intel-hyperthreading.pdf)
- AMD[\[5\]](https://developer.arm.com/community/arm-community-blogs/b/servers-and-cloud-computing-blog/posts/reassess-cpu-utilization-on-x86-and-arm)'s EPYC SMT technology brief points to "often **30%-50%**" gains (while noting minimal power-change) based on cited benchmarking. [\[6\]](https://www.amd.com/content/dam/amd/en/documents/epyc-business-docs/white-papers/amd-epyc-smt-technology-brief.pdf)
- IBM[\[7\]](https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Sally-Mckee/publication/221474560_Understanding_PARSEC_performance_on_contemporary_CMPs/links/09e41512359a8461dd000000/Understanding-PARSEC-performance-on-contemporary-CMPs.pdf) documentation and support content includes a **25%-40%** throughput statement (commercial workloads) and a more explicit discussion claiming **~30%-40% more instructions executed** for SMT=2 and **~40%-60%** for SMT=4 in "typical server workloads," while cautioning that per-thread performance can go down as SMT degree increases. [\[8\]](https://www.ibm.com/docs/en/aix/7.2.0?topic=concepts-simultaneous-multithreading)
- Arm[\[9\]](https://www.cs.princeton.edu/techreports/2007/793.pdf) (serving as a contrast case) publicly states that Neoverse N‑ and V‑series cores **do not implement SMT**, partly to improve predictability and mitigate cross-thread leakage risk-so there is little in the way of "SMT gain" claims from Arm for those lines. [\[10\]](https://developer.arm.com/community/arm-community-blogs/b/servers-and-cloud-computing-blog/posts/reassess-cpu-utilization-on-x86-and-arm)

Academic results (selected exemplars) reinforce that SMT gains are real but uneven:

- On multiprogrammed SPEC[\[11\]](https://pharm.ece.wisc.edu/wddd/2004/06_bulpin.pdf) CPU2000 pairs on a Pentium 4 HT system, one well-cited study reports an **average speedup of 1.20** (≈ **+20%**) with a **range 0.90-1.58** (≈ **−10% to +58%**). [\[12\]](https://users.cs.utah.edu/~rajeev/cs7810/papers/tuck03.pdf)
- A closely related study reports mean ≈ **+20%**, with best and worst cases roughly **+50%** and **−14%** (depending on the co-run pair). [\[13\]](https://pharm.ece.wisc.edu/wddd/2004/06_bulpin.pdf)
- A real-hardware comparison of IBM POWER5 vs Intel Xeon on SPECWeb99 reports **POWER5 +40%-50%** versus **Xeon +10%-30%** for that server workload class. [\[14\]](https://www.cs.princeton.edu/techreports/2007/793.pdf)
- A VLDB database paper reports **+30% to +70% throughput** for in-memory database operations using SMT-aware threading techniques. [\[15\]](https://www1.cs.columbia.edu/~kar/pubsk/vldb2005.pdf)
- For HPC/CFD codes, reported HT benefits range from **none** (one code) to **single‑digit/low‑double‑digit percentages** (e.g., **7%-22%** in one application depending on scale). [\[16\]](https://www.nas.nasa.gov/assets/nas/pdf/papers/saini_s_impact_hyper_threading_2011.pdf)

Putting the heterogeneous set of numeric claims on the same footing (treating "up to X%" as a point at X and range endpoints as separate values), the **median reported gain** across the collected vendor and academic figures is about **+30%**, but the overall range spans from **−14%** (slowdowns) to **+120%** (specialized nested-virtualization speedups leveraging SMT contexts). [\[17\]](https://pharm.ece.wisc.edu/wddd/2004/06_bulpin.pdf)

## What "SMT performance gain" usually means in practice

Most sources-especially vendor and ops-facing guidance-implicitly use "performance" to mean **aggregate work completed per unit time** under concurrency. Examples of explicit throughput-like metrics across sources include:

- "Simultaneous connections" or request-serving capacity in SPECWeb99 (server throughput). [\[14\]](https://www.cs.princeton.edu/techreports/2007/793.pdf)
- "Throughput" for database operations / transactions (DB operations completed per time). [\[15\]](https://www1.cs.columbia.edu/~kar/pubsk/vldb2005.pdf)
- "Instructions executed" as a proxy for throughput under a fixed clock/time window (IBM's guidance). [\[18\]](https://www.ibm.com/support/pages/power-cpu-memory-affinity-3-scheduling-processes-smt-and-virtual-processors)
- Virtualization consolidation/performance comparisons (often "same server with vs without HT"). [\[19\]](https://www.intel.com/content/dam/www/public/us/en/documents/white-papers/virtualization-xeon-core-count-impacts-performance-paper.pdf)

A recurring and important caveat is that SMT tends to trade **per-thread share of core resources** for higher overall utilization:

- IBM's support article states directly that as SMT degree increases, "the number of instructions executed by a single thread … goes down" while overall throughput goes up, and that single-transaction response time "may go up a little." [\[20\]](https://www.ibm.com/support/pages/power-cpu-memory-affinity-3-scheduling-processes-smt-and-virtual-processors)
- The Arm blog highlights that SMT can distort perceived "CPU utilization" because two logical threads share a physical core; this illustrates that accounting conventions can obscure how loaded the underlying core actually is. [\[10\]](https://developer.arm.com/community/arm-community-blogs/b/servers-and-cloud-computing-blog/posts/reassess-cpu-utilization-on-x86-and-arm)

Empirically, studies on SPEC multiprogram workloads show that SMT can deliver large wins for some pairings while producing **flat or negative** outcomes for others, because the two threads may contend for the same bottleneck (e.g., memory bandwidth, cache, or particular execution resources). [\[21\]](https://users.cs.utah.edu/~rajeev/cs7810/papers/tuck03.pdf)

## How each source defines or measures the percentage

**Intel Hyper‑Threading Technology Technical User's Guide** describes a "performance gain of 30 percent" compared with a comparable processor "without Hyper‑Threading Technology," but the excerpt does **not** specify benchmarks, whether this is peak/average, or whether "performance" is throughput versus latency. The claim should be interpreted as a general expectation for multithreaded code, not a guarantee. [\[22\]](https://read.seas.harvard.edu/cs161/2022/pdf/intel-hyperthreading.pdf)

**Intel Technology Journal (2002)** explicitly reports "gains up to 30%" and ties it to "common server application benchmarks," i.e., an application-level reported improvement rather than a microarchitectural metric such as IPC. "Up to" framing implies a best-case figure; typical gains are not enumerated in the excerpt. [\[23\]](https://www.intel.com/content/dam/www/public/us/en/documents/research/2002-vol06-iss-1-intel-technology-journal.pdf)

**Intel timeline entry** states Hyper‑Threading "boosted performance by up to 25%" without specifying the benchmark, whether it is throughput or responsiveness, or the software configuration. Treat this as high-level marketing context rather than a measurement protocol. [\[24\]](https://timeline.intel.com/2002/hyper-threading-the-pentium-4)

**Intel's virtualization white paper** frames the comparison as "the same server without Intel Hyper‑Threading Technology," and states a **10%-20%** boost-again not tied in the excerpt to a particular benchmark name, but clearly scoped to **virtualization** scenarios (where scheduling and vCPU mapping can benefit from additional logical processors). [\[19\]](https://www.intel.com/content/dam/www/public/us/en/documents/white-papers/virtualization-xeon-core-count-impacts-performance-paper.pdf)

**AMD EPYC SMT technology brief** states that "significant SMT performance gains (often 30%-50%) are achieved," while also noting "minimal change in overall power." The excerpt is explicit about the size of the claimed gains, but does not pin the metric to a single benchmark family; it references external benchmarking as evidence. [\[6\]](https://www.amd.com/content/dam/amd/en/documents/epyc-business-docs/white-papers/amd-epyc-smt-technology-brief.pdf)

**IBM AIX documentation** reports "25%-40% increase in throughput" from "measurements… on a dedicated partition with commercial workloads." This is one of the clearer vendor statements because it uses "throughput," but it still does not specify the benchmark suite/workload mix in the excerpt. [\[25\]](https://www.ibm.com/docs/en/aix/7.2.0?topic=concepts-simultaneous-multithreading)

**IBM support article (2023)** gives unusually explicit guidance: for "typical server workloads," SMT=2 yields about **30%-40% improvement in "instructions executed,"** and SMT=4 yields about **40%-60%**-while emphasizing that the improvement depends on instruction mix and that per-thread performance decreases as threads share the core. It also provides an illustrative "SMT boost of 40%" and "SMT boost of 60%" example for throughput metrics like transactions/sec, web hits/sec, or batch completions. [\[32\]](https://www.ibm.com/support/pages/power-cpu-memory-affinity-3-scheduling-processes-smt-and-virtual-processors)

**Arm's blog (2024)** is included because it clarifies that Arm Neoverse N/V "do not implement SMT," therefore eliminating the SMT vs non-SMT ambiguity for those designs. Rather than reporting "SMT gains," it argues that SMT complicates utilization interpretation and can flatten performance scaling at higher utilization due to internal contention. [\[10\]](https://developer.arm.com/community/arm-community-blogs/b/servers-and-cloud-computing-blog/posts/reassess-cpu-utilization-on-x86-and-arm)

**Tuck & Tullsen (PACT 2003)** report multiprogrammed "speedup" for SPEC CPU2000 by running benchmarks in co-scheduled combinations on a Pentium 4 HT system. They summarize the "average multithreaded speedup" for SPEC as **1.20**, ranging **0.90 to 1.58**. Because this is a _system throughput_ style metric (aggregate completion rate of two jobs), it is directly relevant to SMT's primary benefit claim; it also shows potential slowdowns in worst pairing cases. [\[12\]](https://users.cs.utah.edu/~rajeev/cs7810/papers/tuck03.pdf)

**Bulpin (2004)** uses a similar multiprogrammed methodology and reports an average speedup of **1.20**, but with a worst case of **0.86** and best case of **1.50** in their measurements. They emphasize that outcomes vary due to contention (notably memory bus/cache behavior), reinforcing that SMT benefits depend on how co-runners stress shared resources. [\[33\]](https://pharm.ece.wisc.edu/wddd/2004/06_bulpin.pdf)

**Ruan et al. (Princeton TR 2007)** evaluate SMT on two "shipping platforms"-POWER5 and Xeon-using server workloads (SPECWeb99 among them). Their abstract-level summary reports POWER5 delivering **40%-50%** improvement while Xeon delivers **10%-30%**, and their section framing makes clear they are focusing on _throughput improvement when threading is introduced_ rather than single-thread latency. [\[34\]](https://www.cs.princeton.edu/techreports/2007/793.pdf)

**Zhou et al. (VLDB 2005)** study in-memory DB operations, explicitly exploiting SMT with thread-based techniques (including a helper thread approach). Their abstract states that they achieve a **30%-70% throughput improvement** over single-threaded implementations. Because the techniques include algorithmic restructuring (not just "toggle SMT on/off"), the gains are best interpreted as: "SMT enables this throughput increase _when software is adapted to use it effectively_." [\[15\]](https://www1.cs.columbia.edu/~kar/pubsk/vldb2005.pdf)

**Saini et al. (HiPC 2011 / NASA report)** report "percentage performance gain" in HT mode vs ST mode for multiple production codes at multiple scales. They explicitly enumerate gains for Cart3D at several core counts (7%-22%) and note USM3D's gain is ~11% and fairly constant, while OVERFLOW does not benefit. This is a useful counterweight to server-focused narratives: SMT can be modest, neutral, or negative depending on memory behavior, vectorization, and synchronization patterns. [\[35\]](https://www.nas.nasa.gov/assets/nas/pdf/papers/saini_s_impact_hyper_threading_2011.pdf)

**CERN/WLCG benchmarking slides (2017)** explicitly call out that a **+20%** HS06 gain with SMT ON is "not reproduced" by two other benchmark styles (DB12 and KV) in their environment. This is a concrete example of benchmark sensitivity: one suite's uplift does not generalize to another. [\[28\]](https://indico.cern.ch/event/609911/contributions/2620190/attachments/1480455/2295576/WLCG_Workshop_2017_benchmarking_giordano.pdf)

**Vilanova et al. (ISCA 2019)** measures gains from a nested virtualization approach that uses SMT contexts to keep state cached, reporting "up to 1.3× and 1.5× better I/O latency and bandwidth," and "1.2-2.2× speedups on various real-world applications." While not a classic "SMT on vs off" toggle, it is directly framed as leveraging SMT to improve virtualization performance and illustrates that SMT can be an enabler for large improvements in specific OS/hypervisor contexts. [\[29\]](https://www.doc.ic.ac.uk/~lvilanov/publications/files/isca19_svt.pdf)

**Bhadauria et al. (IISWC 2009)** and **Eyerman & Eeckhout (ASPLOS 2014)** both treat SMT as a performance-scaling knob in broader architecture studies (PARSEC-centric in parts). In the excerpts available here, they primarily provide qualitative or normalized results rather than a single "SMT adds X%" number-so they are most useful for _why_ SMT helps (latency hiding, utilizing otherwise-stalled cycles) and for showing that SMT benefits vary by application scaling behavior and core-resource partitioning. [\[36\]](https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Sally-Mckee/publication/221474560_Understanding_PARSEC_performance_on_contemporary_CMPs/links/09e41512359a8461dd000000/Understanding-PARSEC-performance-on-contemporary-CMPs.pdf)

## Cross-vendor synthesis, ranges, and recurring ambiguities

### Reported gain ranges by vendor and overall

To summarize the heterogeneous reporting styles, a consistent comparison requires simplifying assumptions. Here, the statistics below are computed by collecting the explicit numeric percentages in each vendor's materials (including endpoints of ranges and "up to X%" maxima as values), recognizing that this mixes "typical" and "best case" statements.

- **Intel (vendor sources in this report):** min ≈ **10%**, median ≈ **25%**, max ≈ **30%**. [\[37\]](https://www.intel.com/content/dam/www/public/us/en/documents/white-papers/virtualization-xeon-core-count-impacts-performance-paper.pdf)
- **AMD (vendor sources in this report):** range **30%-50%** (only one numeric source captured here, so min/median/max reflect that single range). [\[6\]](https://www.amd.com/content/dam/amd/en/documents/epyc-business-docs/white-papers/amd-epyc-smt-technology-brief.pdf)
- **IBM (vendor sources in this report):** min ≈ **25%**, median ≈ **40%**, max ≈ **60%** (note IBM's 30%-60% numbers are explicitly "instructions executed" regions for SMT=2/4 in typical server workloads). [\[38\]](https://www.ibm.com/docs/en/aix/7.2.0?topic=concepts-simultaneous-multithreading)
- **Arm:** no SMT gains for Neoverse N/V because SMT is not implemented. [\[10\]](https://developer.arm.com/community/arm-community-blogs/b/servers-and-cloud-computing-blog/posts/reassess-cpu-utilization-on-x86-and-arm)

Across **all vendor and academic numeric claims included**, the collected values span **−14% to +120%**, with a **median** around **+30%** (again mixing best-case and typical-style claims). [\[39\]](https://pharm.ece.wisc.edu/wddd/2004/06_bulpin.pdf)

### Why SMT numbers frequently conflict across sources

**Throughput vs single-thread latency.** Many vendor statements are about _aggregate throughput_ (transactions/sec, connections, or "instructions executed"), not per-thread response latency. IBM explicitly spells out that single-thread performance can go down while throughput increases, and that transaction response time may rise slightly even as overall throughput rises. [\[20\]](https://www.ibm.com/support/pages/power-cpu-memory-affinity-3-scheduling-processes-smt-and-virtual-processors)

**"Up to" vs typical/average.** Intel's "up to 30%" phrasing and its timeline "up to 25%" are peak-style claims; they do not specify distribution shape, variance, or the fraction of workloads that see those values. Academic studies on SPEC multiprogramming show why: the same processor can range from slowdowns to large gains depending on co-run pairing. [\[40\]](https://www.intel.com/content/dam/www/public/us/en/documents/research/2002-vol06-iss-1-intel-technology-journal.pdf)

**Workload composition and co-run sensitivity.** Multiprogram results (e.g., SPEC pairs) can show broad variability because co-runners may be complementary or they may bottleneck the same shared resource. The SPEC CPU2000 multiprogram papers emphasize this variability and show both best and worst cases. [\[41\]](https://users.cs.utah.edu/~rajeev/cs7810/papers/tuck03.pdf)

**Microarchitecture differences across vendors and generations.** The same "SMT=2" concept can yield different gains depending on cache sizes, memory bandwidth, and how resources are partitioned/shared. A direct POWER5 vs Xeon comparison on server workloads reports notably higher gains on POWER5 than Xeon, illustrating that "SMT is SMT" is not enough; implementation matters. [\[42\]](https://www.cs.princeton.edu/techreports/2007/793.pdf)

**Benchmark choice.** Even within one environment, SMT can look good on one benchmark and neutral on another. CERN's observation of +20% on HS06 but not on DB12/KV is a concrete example of benchmark dependency. [\[28\]](https://indico.cern.ch/event/609911/contributions/2620190/attachments/1480455/2295576/WLCG_Workshop_2017_benchmarking_giordano.pdf)

**Measurement unit ambiguity.** "Instructions executed" (IBM) is not always the same as user-visible throughput, and "performance gains" (AMD/Intel marketing language) may refer to different aggregates. When a source does not specify metric, a conservative reading is "some aggregate performance measure under multi-threading," not "per-thread IPC improvement." [\[43\]](https://www.ibm.com/support/pages/power-cpu-memory-affinity-3-scheduling-processes-smt-and-virtual-processors)
